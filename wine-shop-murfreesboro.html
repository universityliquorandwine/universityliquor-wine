Wine Education & Resources

Enhance your wine knowledge with our expert guides

Understanding wine labels is essential for selecting the perfect bottle. Here's what to look for:

  • Producer/Winery: The name of the estate or ch√¢teau that made the wine
  • Region: Where the grapes were grown (appellation)
  • Vintage: The year the grapes were harvested
  • Varietal: The type of grape(s) used (e.g., Cabernet Sauvignon)
  • Alcohol Content: Typically 12-15% for most wines
  • Classification: Quality designations like AOC, DOC, or Reserve

European wines often emphasize region, while New World wines typically highlight the grape variety.

Popular Red Varietals:
  • Cabernet Sauvignon: Full-bodied with black currant, cedar, and tobacco notes
  • Merlot: Softer tannins with plum and chocolate flavors
  • Pinot Noir: Lighter body with cherry, raspberry, and earthy notes
  • Syrah/Shiraz: Bold with blackberry, pepper, and smoke
  • Malbec: Rich plum and blackberry with velvety texture
Popular White Varietals:
  • Chardonnay: Can range from crisp citrus to buttery and oaked
  • Sauvignon Blanc: Bright acidity with grapefruit and grass notes
  • Riesling: Aromatic with apple, peach, and floral notes
  • Pinot Grigio: Light and refreshing with citrus and pear
Storage Guidelines:
  • Ideal temperature: 55¬∞F (13¬∞C)
  • Store bottles on their side to keep cork moist
  • Avoid direct sunlight and vibration
  • Maintain consistent temperature and humidity (60-70%)
  • Keep away from strong odors
Serving Temperatures:
  • Sparkling & Champagne: 40-45¬∞F (ice cold)
  • White & Ros√©: 45-50¬∞F (well chilled)
  • Light Reds: 55-60¬∞F (slightly chilled)
  • Full-Bodied Reds: 60-65¬∞F (cool room temperature)
Classic Pairing Principles:
  • Match Weight: Light wines with light dishes, full wines with hearty meals
  • Complement or Contrast: Similar flavors enhance, opposites balance
  • Consider Sauce: Match wine to the sauce more than the protein
  • Regional Pairing: Local food and wine often pair beautifully
Popular Pairings:
  • Cabernet Sauvignon: Grilled steak, lamb, aged cheeses
  • Pinot Noir: Salmon, duck, mushroom dishes
  • Chardonnay: Chicken, seafood, creamy pasta
  • Sauvignon Blanc: Salads, goat cheese, shellfish
  • Champagne: Oysters, fried foods, versatile with most dishes
The Five S's of Wine Tasting:
  1. See: Observe color and clarity. Tilt glass against white background.
  2. Swirl: Gently swirl to aerate and release aromas.
  3. Smell: Take short sniffs to identify fruit, oak, and other notes.
  4. Sip: Take a small sip and let it coat your palate.
  5. Savor: Notice the finish - how long flavors linger after swallowing.
What to Look For:
  • Body: Light, medium, or full
  • Tannins: Astringency (more in reds)
  • Acidity: Brightness and freshness
  • Fruit: Primary flavors (berries, citrus, stone fruit)
  • Balance: Harmony between all elements
What is Vintage?

Vintage refers to the year the grapes were harvested. Weather conditions vary each year, affecting wine quality and character.

Does Older Mean Better?
  • Most wines (90%) are meant to be enjoyed within 1-3 years
  • Only certain wines improve with age (premium Bordeaux, Burgundy, Port)
  • Proper storage is essential for aging wines
  • White wines generally age faster than reds
Wines That Age Well:
  • Bordeaux and Burgundy (5-30+ years)
  • Vintage Port (10-50+ years)
  • Premium Cabernet Sauvignon (5-25 years)
  • Aged Riesling (5-20 years)
  • Champagne (3-15 years for vintage)
